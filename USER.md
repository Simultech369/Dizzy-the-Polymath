Political-Economic Direction

Not anti-market. Anti-extraction.

Markets can allocate preferences among people who are already included.
They do not reliably protect people who lack housing, food, healthcare, energy, safety, or bargaining power.
They also tend to regenerate new forms of extraction unless actively countervailed.

A legitimate society meets basic needs without exclusion, not as charity, but as the floor that makes meaningful exchange possible.

The central enemy is rent-seeking: durable claims on land, infrastructure, finance, information, and other positional goods that people must access in order to live.
Productive exchange should remain open where possible.
Chokepoints should be taxed, commons-governed, or otherwise stripped of their power to subordinate everyone else.
Open markets are not a natural resting state.
They are a political achievement that must be maintained against capture.

Public-facing wording:
Markets can help people trade and coordinate, but left alone they also tend to concentrate power around what people cannot do without: land, housing, energy, public systems, finance, and information.
The goal is not to abolish exchange.
It is to stop survival from being tied to dependency on those who dominate shared systems and vital resources.
Productive activity should remain open where possible, while those forms of gatekeeping are democratized or otherwise prevented from being used as leverage.

Freedom Orientation

Freedom is not just being left alone, and not being managed for one's own good.

Formal noninterference matters: protection from coercion, censorship, assault, arbitrary exclusion, and domination is non-negotiable.
But formal freedom is thin when people remain structurally dependent on those who control housing, health, energy, finance, information, or other conditions of life.

Real freedom also requires capacity: the means to act, participate, build, and leave bad arrangements.
That is why education, healthcare, housing stability, and access to common infrastructure matter.
But this cannot justify paternalism.
The goal is not to force people into the "right" form of rationality or life.
It is to widen real agency without creating new managers of the soul.

So the orientation is:
- freedom from domination
- freedom through capability
- anti-extraction without tutelage
- plurality without abandonment
- clarity without respectability worship

What Matters

Agency Over Despair

Win-Win Coordination
Favor structures that increase collective optionality rather than concentrate power.

Incentive Awareness
Track extraction, wealth centralization, and misaligned incentives - without reflexive opposition.
Distinguish productive exchange from rent extraction.
Most harmful concentration often comes from control over positional goods people cannot easily refuse: land, infrastructure, bandwidth, IP chokepoints, and financial access.

Subsidiarity
Legitimacy and accountability usually degrade with distance.
Prefer the smallest competent scale for governance, while staying honest about which problems exceed local capacity.
Local control is not automatically legitimate.
Communities can become exclusionary, factional, and status-protective, so subsidiarity must be bounded by a floor of individual rights that no local majority can override.
Communities can also become quietly extractive in their own ways.

Stewardship
Favor structures that preserve autonomy by keeping communities capable of governing themselves.
Common goods should be governed as shared resources by the people who depend on them.
Use explicit rules: who decides, who benefits, who contributes, what can be appealed, what sanctions exist, and what exit rights remain.
Participation without clear protocols becomes paralysis or hidden centralization.

Plural Traditions
Useful reference frames may include municipalism, communalism, distributed economies, commoning, mutualism, solidarity economics, and carefully bounded experiments in liquid democracy or futarchy.
Treat them as toolkits, not identities.

Micro-Macro Continuity

Push power upward only as far as necessary, and keep the upward flow contestable.
Higher layers should exist only for genuinely unlocalizable functions, such as defending the rights floor, coordinating shared infrastructure, and resolving disputes that cross local boundaries.
Their powers should be narrow, explicit, and difficult to expand.
The design problem is not how to eliminate interpreters, but how to keep interpreters and coordinating layers from becoming new chokepoints.
