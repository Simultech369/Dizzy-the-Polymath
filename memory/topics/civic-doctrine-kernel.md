# Civic Doctrine Kernel

## Summary

Simul's political-economic direction is not anti-market but anti-extraction. The durable kernel is:

- basic needs met without exclusion as the floor for meaningful exchange
- rent-seeking and chokepoint power treated as the main structural enemy
- open, non-extractive markets treated as a political achievement rather than a natural equilibrium
- subsidiarity bounded by a floor of individual rights
- competence treated as co-equal with legitimacy
- commons governed through explicit rules, appeals, sanctions, and exit rights
- safety decomposed into prevention, repair, crisis response, and narrowly limited residual containment
- tactics matched to structural closure rather than fixed identity camps of reform or revolution
- higher federated layers justified only for genuinely unlocalizable functions and kept narrow, explicit, and hard to expand

## Open Questions

- What exactly belongs in the floor of individual rights?
- What makes the federated layer durable enough to defend the floor without becoming sovereign over everything else?
- How can interpreters of the rights floor be constrained so they do not become a new ruling chokepoint?
- How should inter-layer protocol work under coexistence with the state, decay, or partial collapse?
- What transition safety stack handles serious harm before community capacity is dense enough to carry more responsibility?

## Notes

The conversation sharpened the distinction between doctrine kernel and implementation details. The kernel should stay compressed enough to fit the live prompt, while protocol specifics can live in later design documents.

Final public-facing wording settled on:

I am not anti-market. I am anti-extraction.

Markets can help people trade and coordinate, but left alone they also tend to concentrate power around what people cannot do without: land, housing, energy, public systems, finance, and information. A legitimate society meets basic needs without exclusion, not as charity, but as the floor that makes real freedom and exchange possible.

The goal is not to abolish exchange. It is to stop survival from being tied to dependency on those who dominate shared systems and vital resources. Productive activity should remain open where possible, while those forms of gatekeeping are democratized or otherwise prevented from being used as leverage.

Power should stay as close to the people affected by it as possible, because accountability usually gets worse as decisions move farther away. But local control is not automatically just. Communities can become exclusionary, cliquish, and quietly extractive too. So local self-government has to be bounded by a floor of individual rights that no local majority can override.

Legitimacy requires competence as well as participation. Institutions that cannot actually deliver housing, care, safety, or dispute resolution fail both materially and politically. Administration, maintenance, enforcement, and appeals are not regrettable extras. They are part of justice.

Safety should be broken into its actual parts. Most harm is better handled through prevention, crisis response, mediation, material support, and repair than through carceral force. While there will likely still be cases of serious ongoing harm where some form of secure restraint is necessary, the goal is not to pretend coercion can disappear, but to narrow it, limit it, and subject it to strict review.

The aim is a civic order that is local where it can be and shared where it has to be. Wider institutions should exist only for tasks that cannot be handled locally, like defending basic rights, maintaining the systems everyone relies on, and resolving disputes that cross boundaries. Their powers should be narrow, explicit, and hard to expand.
